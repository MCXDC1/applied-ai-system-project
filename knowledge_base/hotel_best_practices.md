# Pet Hotel Best Practices and Daily Operations

## Intake Assessment on Arrival
Every pet must complete an intake assessment before being placed in the general hotel area. Record: species, breed, age, weight, vaccination dates (rabies, DHPP for dogs; FVRCP, rabies for cats), spay/neuter status, temperament with other animals and people, feeding schedule and food provided, medications with full instructions, emergency contact details, and authorization for emergency veterinary care. Take a photo of the pet on arrival to document physical condition at check-in. Flag any health concerns, behavioral notes, or special needs in a visible location in the pet's housing area.

## Daily Schedule Structure for Pet Hotels
A well-run pet hotel day follows a consistent structure: 7:00–8:00 AM: morning feeding, medication round, litter cleaning, exercise start; 8:00–10:00 AM: group play (dogs), interactive enrichment (cats); 10:00 AM–12:00 PM: individual walks and outdoor time; 12:00–2:00 PM: midday rest and quiet time — minimize stimulation; 2:00–4:00 PM: individual enrichment, grooming checks, afternoon walks; 4:00–6:00 PM: group play or supervised socialization; 5:00–6:00 PM: evening feeding and medication round; 6:00–8:00 PM: wind-down enrichment, final bathroom breaks; 8:00–9:00 PM: bed preparation, lights dim, settling animals for night.

## Group Play Safety Guidelines
Conduct a temperament test for all new dogs before group play introduction. Separate groups by size: small dogs (under 25 lbs), medium dogs (25–55 lbs), large dogs (over 55 lbs). Do not mix intact and altered adults in the same group. Maintain a ratio of no more than 6–8 dogs per staff member during group play. Keep group play sessions to 30–60 minutes with breaks in between. Intervene immediately if any dog shows resource guarding, intense staring, stiff body posture, or attempts to mount excessively. End play sessions before dogs become overtired — tired dogs are more likely to have altercations.

## Enrichment Activity Schedule
Physical enrichment: leash walks, outdoor sniff walks, fetch, agility-style play. Mental enrichment: puzzle feeders, snuffle mats, Kong toys stuffed with food, hide-and-seek with treats, scent games. Social enrichment: group play (for social animals), staff interaction time, guided interaction with calm compatible animals. Cats: wand toys, feather teasers, window bird watching, cat tunnels, cardboard boxes. Rotate toys and activities daily to maintain novelty. Schedule enrichment sessions in the late morning and late afternoon to align with natural activity peaks.

## Isolation and Quarantine Protocols
Any animal showing signs of illness (vomiting, diarrhea, nasal discharge, coughing, limping) must be isolated from other guests immediately. Designate a separate isolation area with its own ventilation and cleaning supplies. Use separate staff or change gloves and wash hands between isolation and general care. Alert the owner within 2 hours of isolating their pet. Consult the owner on whether to seek veterinary care. Animals in quarantine should receive the same food, medication, and care schedule with enhanced health monitoring.

## Cleaning and Sanitation Standards
Clean and disinfect all housing areas daily: spot clean throughout the day, deep clean once daily with a pet-safe disinfectant (avoid phenol-based cleaners, especially near cats). Wash food and water bowls in hot soapy water at every meal. Wash bedding every 2–3 days or immediately if soiled. Mop floors with pet-safe disinfectant at least once per day. Change litter boxes twice daily (scoop), full replacement every 3–4 days. Disinfect toys and enrichment items every 2–3 days. Log all cleaning activities with time and staff signature.

## Staff Communication and Shift Handover
Every shift handover must include a verbal briefing and a written log review covering: any animals with health concerns, medication changes, behavioral incidents, dietary changes, and upcoming check-ins or checkouts. Use a shared digital or physical log book where staff document all significant events (refused food, medication administered, injury, behavioral concern) as they occur — not at the end of shift. Post each pet's key information (name, species, feeding instructions, medications, behavioral notes) visibly on or near their housing area.

## Emergency Procedures
Post emergency veterinary clinic contact information (primary and after-hours) in every care area. If an animal shows signs of acute medical emergency (respiratory distress, collapse, seizure, suspected poisoning, severe injury), call the veterinarian immediately while another staff member calls the owner. Do not delay emergency care waiting for owner authorization if the animal is in critical danger — have a signed emergency care authorization on file at intake. Document the timeline of events and actions taken for every incident. After any emergency, conduct a team debrief to review what happened and improve future protocols.

## Owner Communication Standards
Send a check-in confirmation message to owners on the day of arrival. Provide a daily update (photo + brief note) for stays longer than two nights. Contact owners promptly (within 2 hours during business hours) for: refusal to eat for more than one meal, illness symptoms, injury, behavioral concerns, or medication questions. At checkout, provide a written summary of the stay including: food consumed, any health observations, behavioral notes, and the completed medication log if applicable. Positive updates and photos build trust and reduce owner anxiety during separation.

## Check-Out Procedures and Documentation
Check-out should be completed during designated hours (typically 8:00 AM–12:00 PM) to allow sufficient time for a thorough handover before afternoon activities begin. At check-out, a staff member must walk the owner through a written stay summary that includes: all meals served and quantities consumed, medications administered with exact times, any behavioral incidents or health observations, enrichment activities completed, and the pet's current condition. Return all owner-supplied items (food containers, medication, bedding, toys) in a labeled bag and confirm each item against the intake checklist. Take a brief exit-condition photo of the pet before handover — this documents the pet's physical state at the time of departure and protects the facility against post-departure health claims. Collect payment and any outstanding signatures before the pet leaves the facility, and issue a receipt with the complete itemized stay record.

## Noise and Stress Management During Busy Periods
During high-traffic periods (weekend check-ins, holidays, early morning feeding rounds), noise levels in housing areas can spike rapidly and create a cascade of anxiety responses across multiple animals. Designate quiet zones in the facility where noise-sensitive animals (elderly pets, cats, small animals) are housed away from high-traffic dog runs and entry areas. Play species-appropriate calming audio — classical music or white noise at a low volume has been shown to reduce cortisol in kenneled dogs — during peak noise periods. Stagger feeding and handling rounds for different species so that the activity level in any single area does not peak all at once. If barking escalates during a busy period, address the triggering animal first (remove from visual contact with other dogs, offer a stuffed Kong, or move to a quieter space) rather than waiting for the noise to self-resolve, as sustained group barking elevates stress in all animals present.

## Temperature and Environment Monitoring
Check and log temperature and humidity levels in all housing areas at least three times daily: at the start of morning shift (around 7:00 AM), midday (around 1:00 PM), and end of evening shift (around 8:00 PM). Ideal ambient temperature for dogs and cats is 65–75°F; humidity should be maintained between 30–70%. Brachycephalic breeds (Bulldogs, Pugs, French Bulldogs, Persian cats) are highly vulnerable to heat stress and must be housed in the cooler end of the acceptable range, never above 70°F in their immediate area. Small animals have narrower acceptable temperature ranges: rabbits must be kept below 75°F, reptiles require habitat-specific gradients confirmed with the owner. If HVAC failure or outdoor heat causes temperatures to approach unsafe levels, activate contingency cooling protocols (portable fans, cool water-soaked towels for dogs, moving animals to the coolest available room) and alert the facility manager immediately.

## Managing Last-Minute and Emergency Check-Ins
Last-minute or emergency check-ins (same-day requests, walk-ins during peak hours) must be handled without bypassing any intake steps — a shortened intake increases risk for all animals in the facility. Designate a specific staff member during peak hours as the intake coordinator so that last-minute arrivals do not pull staff away from animals already in care. If the owner cannot provide vaccination records at drop-off, place the new arrival in a temporary isolation area until records are confirmed — do not introduce an unverified pet to the general population. For genuine emergencies (owner hospitalization, natural disaster evacuation), collect as much medical and dietary history as possible by phone or from whoever is dropping off the pet, and flag the record for follow-up as soon as the owner is reachable. Charge last-minute surcharges consistently and document the policy in writing — this is disclosed at intake, not after.

## Photo and Update Communication Schedule for Owners
Establish a predictable communication rhythm so owners know when to expect updates rather than waiting anxiously. For stays of one night, send one check-in photo in the evening of the arrival day. For stays of two to seven nights, send one photo update and a two-to-three sentence note daily, ideally between 11:00 AM and 1:00 PM when morning activities are complete and the animal is typically relaxed. For stays longer than seven nights, supplement daily photos with a brief weekly summary covering feeding patterns, activity levels, and any health observations. Photos should show the pet engaged in an activity or resting comfortably — avoid images that could alarm owners (e.g., an animal looking stressed, inside a crate, or mid-bark). All communication should be logged with timestamp so that staff across shifts know what has already been sent to each owner.

## End-of-Day Wind-Down Routine
A consistent wind-down routine beginning at approximately 7:30–8:00 PM signals to animals that the day is ending and promotes faster settling for overnight rest. Begin by reducing ambient lighting in housing areas to a dim level and lowering background noise. Complete final bathroom breaks for all dogs between 7:30 PM and 8:30 PM, in small calm groups rather than high-energy group runs. Distribute final enrichment items — a stuffed Kong, a chew, or a food puzzle — after the last bathroom break so animals have something calming to engage with as they settle. Perform a final health and safety check of every housing area by 9:00 PM: confirm each animal has fresh water, is displaying normal behavior, is not in physical distress, and that all enclosure latches and run gates are securely closed. Log the completion of the wind-down check with the staff member's initials and time before the evening shift ends.
