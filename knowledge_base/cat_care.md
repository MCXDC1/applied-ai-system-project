# Cat Care Guidelines for Pet Hotels

## Feeding Schedule for Adult Cats
Adult cats should be fed twice daily — morning and evening — or with a timed puzzle feeder if the owner requests free-feeding. Wet food should not sit out for more than 30 minutes to prevent spoilage. Dry food can be left available for cats that free-feed. Always provide fresh water in a clean bowl; many cats prefer running water and a small fountain can increase hydration. Do not mix foods from different brands or proteins without a gradual transition as sudden changes cause digestive upset.

## Litter Box Maintenance in Hotel Settings
In a hotel setting, each cat should ideally have their own litter box. At minimum, maintain one litter box per two cats in a shared space. Scoop at least twice daily — morning and evening — and completely replace litter every 3–4 days. Cats are highly sensitive to a dirty litter box and may refuse to use it, leading to inappropriate elimination. Use unscented litter unless the owner specifies otherwise. Covered boxes are preferred by some cats but can trap odors — check and clean more frequently.

## Environmental Enrichment for Cats
Cats need vertical space — provide cat trees, shelving, or elevated perches so they can observe their environment from height. Hiding spots are critical for stress reduction; always offer a covered bed or box where the cat can retreat. Rotate toys to maintain interest: feather wands, crinkle balls, catnip toys, and laser pointers. Schedule at least two 10–15 minute interactive play sessions per day to meet hunting instincts. Window access with a bird feeder view provides significant enrichment for indoor cats.

## Stress Reduction for Cats in New Environments
Cats are highly territorial and may be significantly stressed in a hotel environment. On arrival, allow the cat to remain in its carrier for 15–30 minutes to acclimate before opening. Provide a pheromone diffuser (Feliway or equivalent) in cat housing areas to reduce anxiety. Play soft background music or white noise to mask sudden sounds. Avoid placing cats within sight or smell of dogs where possible. Cats that are hiding should not be forced out — allow them to emerge on their own schedule.

## Cat Socialization Guidelines
Most cats are solitary and should not be housed with unfamiliar cats unless the owner confirms they are bonded companions. Even cats that live together may behave differently in a new environment and should be monitored closely if housed together. Introduce unfamiliar cats slowly using scent swapping (exchange bedding) before any visual contact. Never force physical interaction between cats. Signs of incompatibility: hissing, growling, swatting, and sustained staring — separate immediately.

## Breed-Specific Cat Care
Persians and Himalayans have flat faces (brachycephalic) and long fur requiring daily grooming to prevent mat formation around the face and belly. Maine Coons are large, active, and social; they do well with enrichment activities and can be leash-trained. Siamese cats are very vocal and highly social — isolation causes distress; they need frequent interaction. Bengal cats are high-energy and need significant daily play. Ragdolls are docile and go limp when held, making them easy to handle but also prone to not alerting staff when in pain.

## Grooming Requirements for Cats
Short-haired cats (Domestic Shorthair, Abyssinian) need brushing only once or twice per week. Medium and long-haired cats (Maine Coon, Norwegian Forest Cat, Persian) require daily brushing to prevent mats. Check for mats behind ears, under armpits, and at the base of the tail. Never cut mats with scissors without proper training; use a dematting comb or consult a groomer. Cats with flat faces (Persians) need daily eye discharge cleaning with a damp cloth to prevent skin fold infections.

## Signs of Stress or Illness in Cats
Stress indicators: hiding for extended periods, loss of appetite beyond 24 hours, over-grooming or pulling fur, aggression toward staff, vomiting, inappropriate elimination outside the litter box. Signs requiring immediate veterinary attention: labored or open-mouth breathing (always an emergency in cats), complete refusal to eat for more than 24–36 hours, straining to urinate (especially in male cats — can be fatal), blood in urine or stool, sudden collapse, seizures, or high fever. Urinary blockage in male cats is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate veterinary care.

## Cat Rest and Sleep Needs
Cats sleep 12–16 hours per day and need uninterrupted rest periods. Do not disturb sleeping cats unnecessarily. Schedule active play during their naturally active periods: dawn (6:00–8:00 AM) and dusk (5:00–7:00 PM). Provide soft, warm bedding as cats prefer warmth. Older cats sleep even more and should be checked regularly if immobile for long periods.

## Overnight Monitoring for Cats
Perform a quiet visual check on all cats at 10:30 PM and again at 5:30 AM to confirm normal resting posture and verify that food and water have been appropriately consumed since the evening round. Cats that are pacing, vocalizing persistently, or pressing their heads against surfaces after lights-out may be experiencing pain or neurological distress and must be flagged for veterinary evaluation. Male cats must be specifically checked for litter box use during the overnight window, as urinary obstruction can develop rapidly and silently during low-staff hours. Ensure each cat's sleeping space maintains a temperature of 70–75°F, as cats are particularly sensitive to cold drafts at night. Document any cat that did not use its litter box between the 8:00 PM and 5:30 AM checks.

## Multi-Cat Household Pets Adjusting to Solo Stays
Cats from multi-cat households may experience significant social stress when housed alone, as they have adapted to constant companionship and ambient social stimulation. Increase interactive staff contact for these cats to at least three 10-minute sessions per day — morning (8:00 AM), midday (1:00 PM), and evening (6:00 PM) — to partially compensate for the absence of feline housemates. Provide an article of clothing or a blanket from home that carries the scent of their companion cats, as familiar scents reduce cortisol levels in socially bonded cats. Monitor food intake closely; socially stressed cats from bonded pairs are more likely to stop eating within 24–48 hours of arrival than cats accustomed to solo living. Note any change in behavior toward staff — unusual clinginess or increased vocalization is a normal adjustment response and should be logged but not cause for alarm unless accompanied by appetite loss.

## Dental and Oral Health Checks
During each morning feeding at 7:00–8:00 AM, observe the cat eating for signs of oral discomfort such as dropping food, chewing on one side only, drooling, or pawing at the mouth. If the owner has consented to a brief oral inspection, gently lift the lips with a clean gloved finger and note the gum color (healthy gums are pink and moist), the presence of excessive tartar buildup, inflamed gum lines, or broken teeth. Cats with known dental disease may require wet food only — confirm this requirement at intake and document it in the feeding log. Do not attempt to brush a cat's teeth during a hotel stay without explicit owner authorization and a calm, compliant cat. Oral pain can cause behavioral changes including increased hiding, food refusal, and uncharacteristic aggression — report these signs to the owner promptly.

## Weight and Body Condition Monitoring During Stays
Cats staying for more than four nights should be weighed on day one of arrival and again every three days using a digital pet scale, recording weight in the intake log. A loss of more than 5% of body weight during a stay is clinically significant and warrants an owner call and possible veterinary consultation. Compare food offered versus food consumed at each meal by weighing or visually assessing the remainder; a cat consistently leaving more than half its food warrants increased monitoring. Body condition scoring (BCS on a 1–9 scale) should be noted at intake for cats staying over seven days, as it provides a baseline for detecting physical decline. Weight loss in cats can rapidly lead to hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease), which becomes a medical emergency after 48–72 hours of significantly reduced food intake.

## Play Session Timing by Time of Day
Morning play sessions (7:30–9:00 AM) should leverage the cat's natural post-dawn activity surge with active wand toys, feather teasers, and chase-based play that mimics hunting. Midday sessions (12:00–2:00 PM) coincide with the cat's lowest activity period — offer calm enrichment such as puzzle feeders, treat-hiding games, or gentle brushing rather than high-energy toys. Evening sessions (5:30–7:30 PM) represent the second peak of crepuscular activity and are the best time for the most engaging interactive play, aiming for 10–15 minutes of sustained engagement to promote natural fatigue before lights-out. Avoid intense play within 30 minutes of feeding to prevent regurgitation, particularly in cats that eat rapidly. Rotate toy types between sessions so that the same toy is not offered more than once per day, as cats habituate quickly and lose interest in repeated stimuli.

## Post-Anesthesia and Post-Procedure Care
Cats recovering from anesthesia or a veterinary procedure must be housed in a quiet, low-stimulation room with a soft non-slip surface at floor level to prevent falls during the recovery period. Monitor every 30 minutes for the first 4 hours post-procedure for signs of incomplete recovery: staggering, prolonged hypothermia (check ear or rectal temperature if available), excessive vocalization, respiratory difficulty, or failure to regain normal posture within 2–3 hours. Do not offer food or water until the cat can hold its head upright and swallow voluntarily — typically 2–4 hours post-anesthesia — then offer only a small amount of water first. Keep the recovering cat separated from all other animals and from high-traffic staff areas for a minimum of 12 hours. Contact the supervising veterinarian immediately if the cat has not returned to a fully alert state within 6 hours of the recorded procedure end time.
