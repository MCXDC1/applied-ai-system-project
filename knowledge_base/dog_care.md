# Dog Care Guidelines for Pet Hotels

## Feeding Schedule for Adult Dogs
Adult dogs should be fed twice daily — once in the morning (7:00–8:00 AM) and once in the evening (5:00–6:00 PM). Portions should follow the owner's instructions or the feeding guide on the food packaging based on the dog's weight. Always provide fresh water throughout the day. Never feed dogs within 30 minutes before or after vigorous exercise to reduce the risk of bloat, especially in large and deep-chested breeds.

## Feeding Schedule for Puppies (Under 1 Year)
Puppies under 6 months require three to four meals per day. Puppies aged 6–12 months can transition to two to three meals per day. Small breed puppies are prone to hypoglycemia and must not go more than 4–5 hours without food. Always consult the owner's instructions for exact portion sizes, as overfeeding puppies can cause developmental joint problems, especially in large breeds.

## Exercise Needs by Breed Size
Small breeds (Chihuahuas, Shih Tzus, Maltese) need 20–30 minutes of light activity per day — short walks and indoor play are sufficient. Medium breeds (Beagles, Bulldogs, Cocker Spaniels) need 30–45 minutes of moderate exercise. Large breeds (Golden Retrievers, Labradors, German Shepherds) require 60–90 minutes of vigorous daily activity. Giant breeds (Great Danes, Saint Bernards) need moderate exercise — 30–45 minutes — but should avoid high-impact activities to protect their joints.

## High-Energy Breed Care (Border Collies, Huskies, Vizslas)
High-energy working breeds require both physical and mental stimulation. Schedule at least 60–90 minutes of active exercise split across the day. Without adequate stimulation these dogs may exhibit destructive behavior or excessive barking. Puzzle feeders, scent games, and structured play sessions help burn mental energy. These breeds do poorly in isolation and benefit from supervised group play.

## Brachycephalic Breed Care (Bulldogs, Pugs, Boston Terriers, French Bulldogs)
Brachycephalic (flat-faced) dogs are prone to overheating and respiratory distress. Limit exercise to early morning or evening when temperatures are cooler. Avoid strenuous activity entirely in warm or humid conditions. Always provide shade and cool water. Watch for signs of heat stress: excessive panting, drooling, pale gums, or collapse. These breeds should never be left in warm environments or allowed to over-exert.

## Grooming by Coat Type
Short-coated dogs (Boxers, Dalmatians) need minimal grooming — a weekly brush to remove loose hair. Double-coated breeds (Huskies, German Shepherds, Corgis) shed heavily and should be brushed daily or every other day during a hotel stay to minimize matting and shedding. Long-coated breeds (Shih Tzus, Maltese, Afghan Hounds) require daily brushing to prevent tangles. Check ears daily for redness or odor in floppy-eared breeds (Basset Hounds, Cocker Spaniels) as they are prone to infections.

## Group Play Compatibility
Not all dogs are suitable for group play. Screen all dogs on arrival using a temperament assessment. Separate by size to prevent accidental injury — small dogs should not play with large dogs unsupervised. Do not include intact (unneutered/unspayed) adults in group play as they can trigger aggression. Separate immediately at signs of resource guarding, stiff posture, sustained staring, or raised hackles. Always have at least one staff member per six dogs during group play.

## Socialization and Alone Time
Most dogs should have structured alone time during the day to prevent over-stimulation and exhaustion. Provide a quiet rest period of 2–3 hours midday. Dogs that show signs of separation anxiety (whining, pacing, destructive behavior) should be given comfort items from home (blanket, toy) and checked on more frequently. Dogs new to the hotel environment may need a quiet settling period of 30–60 minutes before joining group activities.

## Signs of Stress or Illness in Dogs
Common stress indicators: yawning, lip licking, whale eye (showing whites of eyes), tucked tail, flattened ears, excessive panting without heat, pacing, refusal to eat. Signs requiring immediate veterinary attention: vomiting more than twice, bloody stool or urine, labored breathing, collapse, severe lethargy, seizures, pale or blue gums, swollen abdomen. Always document and notify the owner promptly if any health concern arises.

## Breed-Specific Considerations
Golden Retrievers and Labradors are typically friendly and social but prone to overeating — do not allow food-sharing. German Shepherds can be protective and may need slower socialization introductions. Chihuahuas may be fear-aggressive despite small size. Dachshunds have fragile spines — avoid stairs and jumping. Greyhounds have low body fat and are cold-sensitive; provide blankets. Pit Bull type dogs may have dog-selective tendencies and should be assessed carefully before group play.

## Overnight Care and Night Check-Ins
Staff must perform a visual check on all dogs at 10:00 PM and again at 2:00 AM to verify that each animal is resting comfortably and has not experienced distress, injury, or illness since the evening feeding. Dogs showing signs of restlessness, whining, or pacing after 10:00 PM should be noted in the overnight log and checked every 30 minutes until settled. Puppies under 6 months and senior dogs over 10 years require a dedicated check at 4:00 AM due to their higher risk of overnight hypoglycemia and disorientation. Ensure all water bowls are refilled during the 10:00 PM round. Any dog that has not urinated since the 8:00 PM walk must be taken outside before lights-out at 11:00 PM.

## Water Intake Monitoring
Fresh water must be available to every dog at all times and bowls must be refilled at a minimum during morning setup (7:00 AM), midday (12:00 PM), and evening rounds (6:00 PM). Staff should note and log any dog that refuses water for more than 8 hours, as dehydration can escalate quickly in active or anxious animals. Dogs on diuretic medications or with kidney disease may require more frequent water monitoring and should have intake estimated and recorded each shift. Increase water availability and observation frequency during warm weather or following vigorous exercise sessions. Signs of dehydration include dry gums, skin tenting (skin that does not spring back quickly when pinched), sunken eyes, and lethargy — report these immediately.

## Thunderstorm and Anxiety Protocols
When a thunderstorm or fireworks event is forecasted or occurring, move all outdoor dogs inside no later than 30 minutes before the predicted start time. Provide anxious dogs with a quiet interior room away from windows, as glass amplifies pressure changes and sound. Approved calming aids (owner-supplied Adaptil spray, anxiety wraps such as ThunderShirts, or veterinarian-prescribed medications) should be applied 20–30 minutes before the anticipated trigger event. Staff must not leave dogs unattended during active thunderstorms; maintain one staff member in the kennel area at all times during the event. Document start and end times of the anxiety episode, any interventions used, and the dog's response for the owner report.

## Post-Surgery and Injury Recovery Care
Dogs recovering from surgery or injury must be housed in a restricted-activity kennel run and not permitted in group play for the duration of their recovery as specified by the attending veterinarian. Elizabethan collars (e-collars) must remain on at all times unless being directly supervised during feeding. Exercise must be limited to short 5–10 minute leash-only toilet walks, no more than three times daily, avoiding stairs and uneven terrain. Incision sites must be inspected at each walk and at the 10:00 PM night check — note any redness, swelling, discharge, or signs of the dog chewing at the site. All post-operative or recovery instructions must be filed with the pet's intake record and followed exactly; contact the owner's veterinarian if any ambiguity arises.

## Inter-Dog Introduction Procedures for Hotel Arrivals
All new dog arrivals must undergo a 15–30 minute decompression period in an isolated quiet kennel before any contact with resident dogs or group play areas. After decompression, conduct a parallel walk introduction outdoors — walk the new dog and a calm resident dog side by side at a distance of 6–10 feet, allowing them to become aware of each other without direct contact. Reduce the distance gradually over 10–15 minutes only if both dogs remain relaxed (loose body language, soft gaze, natural gait). Direct nose-to-nose greetings should be brief (under 3 seconds) and interrupted before tension can build. Dogs that show any reactivity during the parallel walk must be kept separated and flagged as not suitable for group play during this stay.

## Daily Enrichment Timing Windows
Morning enrichment (8:30–10:00 AM) should focus on active, higher-energy activities such as fetch, agility recall games, or scent trails to burn off overnight energy. Midday enrichment (12:30–2:00 PM) should shift to calmer mental stimulation such as Kong stuffing, sniff mats, or puzzle feeders during the post-lunch rest window. Afternoon enrichment (3:30–5:00 PM) can include moderate group play or structured leash exploration of novel areas of the facility. Rotate enrichment types daily to prevent habituation and maintain interest. Document which enrichment activities were offered and the dog's engagement level so that owners receive a meaningful summary at checkout.
