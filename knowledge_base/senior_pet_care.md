# Senior Pet Care Guidelines for Pet Hotels

## Defining Senior Age Thresholds
Dogs are considered senior at 7 years for small and medium breeds, 6 years for large breeds (over 50 lbs), and 5 years for giant breeds (over 90 lbs). Cats are considered senior at 10 years and geriatric at 15 years. Senior pets have different physiological needs and require additional monitoring, modified activity levels, and often dietary adjustments. Always flag senior status in the daily schedule to ensure staff give appropriate care and attention.

## Physical Accommodations for Senior Pets
Senior dogs and cats with arthritis or joint stiffness need orthopedic or memory foam bedding — avoid hard surfaces. Provide ramps or steps instead of requiring jumping to reach elevated resting spots. Senior dogs may need assistance getting in and out of vehicles or raised surfaces. Keep senior pets on the ground floor where possible. Ensure the sleeping area is warm and away from drafts, as older animals are less able to thermoregulate. Non-slip mats on smooth floors help dogs with reduced mobility and prevent falls.

## Modified Exercise for Senior Dogs
Senior dogs still need daily movement but with reduced intensity and duration. Replace high-impact running with gentle leash walks. Two to three short walks of 10–20 minutes each are better than one long walk for senior dogs with joint issues. Avoid steep hills, stairs, or rough terrain. Allow the dog to set the pace — never force activity if the dog is reluctant. Gentle stretching and massage before and after walks reduces stiffness. Swimming is excellent low-impact exercise for senior dogs if facilities are available.

## Rest Requirements for Senior Pets
Senior dogs sleep 12–14 hours per day; senior cats may sleep up to 18–20 hours. Do not disturb senior pets unnecessarily during rest periods. Their sleep is restorative and important for immune function. Schedule check-ins every 2–3 hours rather than waiting for group activity periods. Senior pets may be disoriented when woken suddenly — approach calmly and speak before touching.

## Dietary Considerations for Senior Pets
Senior-formula dog and cat food has reduced calorie density (to prevent obesity) and modified protein and phosphorus levels to support kidney function. Do not switch a senior pet's food without owner instructions — sudden diet changes in older animals cause significant digestive upset. Senior pets may have dental disease making dry kibble painful; check with the owner if wet food is preferred. Monitor food intake closely — appetite changes in senior pets can indicate pain, nausea, or illness. Ensure adequate hydration; senior cats especially are prone to chronic kidney disease and dehydration.

## Medication Management for Senior Pets
Senior pets are more likely to be on daily medications for conditions including arthritis (NSAIDs), heart disease, thyroid conditions, diabetes, or anxiety. Confirm medication schedules on check-in and document exact dosing times. Missing a dose of certain medications (especially insulin for diabetic pets or heart medications) can be dangerous. Keep medications in a clearly labeled container separate from each pet's belongings. Never administer a medication not listed on the intake form without owner or veterinary authorization.

## Cognitive Dysfunction in Senior Pets
Canine and feline cognitive dysfunction syndrome (similar to dementia in humans) affects many pets over 10 years. Signs include disorientation in familiar environments, staring at walls, getting stuck in corners, disturbed sleep/wake cycles, increased vocalization at night, and loss of house-training. Senior pets with cognitive dysfunction need a calm, consistent routine. Night check-ins may be necessary if the pet vocalizes or becomes distressed. Notify the owner if new cognitive symptoms appear during the stay.

## Health Monitoring for Senior Pets
Monitor and document appetite, water consumption, urination frequency, stool consistency, and activity level at minimum twice daily. Senior dogs may need more frequent bathroom breaks (every 3–4 hours) to accommodate reduced bladder control. Signs requiring immediate veterinary attention: sudden collapse, difficulty breathing, unresponsiveness, severe vomiting, or inability to urinate. Subtle signs that warrant owner notification: reduced appetite, increased thirst, limping, confusion, or unusual behavior changes. Early detection of problems is critical in senior animals as they can decline rapidly.

## Emotional Well-Being of Senior Pets
Senior pets are often strongly bonded to their routines and their owners. A hotel stay can be more stressful for seniors than for younger animals. Provide comfort items from home (familiar blanket, toy, or piece of owner's clothing) to reduce anxiety. Play soft calming music or white noise. Limit exposure to loud, chaotic environments. Extra gentle interaction from staff — quiet talking, gentle stroking — provides significant comfort to senior pets and reduces stress hormones.

## Pain Assessment and Mobility Monitoring
Staff should observe senior pets for behavioral signs of pain during every interaction, including reluctance to rise, guarding a limb, flinching when touched, unusual aggression, or changes in posture. A simple 0–3 pain scale (0 = no signs, 1 = mild reluctance, 2 = clear discomfort, 3 = distress or refusal to move) should be noted in the daily log. Assess mobility at the start of morning walks — note whether the pet warms up and moves more freely after a few minutes, which is characteristic of arthritis, versus steady or worsening lameness. Any pet scoring 2 or above on the pain scale should be kept on strict rest, and the owner should be notified that same day. Document the time each observation was made so the owner and their veterinarian can identify patterns across multi-day stays.

## Senior Dental Care During Stays
Many senior pets have some degree of dental disease, which can cause chronic pain affecting eating, behavior, and systemic health. On check-in, note whether the owner has mentioned dental issues and whether the pet is on any oral medications or rinses. Observe the pet's eating style — dropping food, chewing only on one side, or slow eating can indicate oral pain and should be documented. Do not attempt to brush teeth without explicit owner authorization and demonstration; improper brushing on sensitive gums can cause pain and distrust. If significant drooling, pawing at the mouth, foul odor, or refusal to eat is observed, notify the owner and recommend a veterinary evaluation promptly.

## Incontinence Management and Bathroom Schedules
Bladder and bowel incontinence is common in senior dogs and some senior cats, and should be treated with patience and without any form of reprimand. Senior dogs with suspected incontinence should be taken outside every 2–3 hours as a standard schedule, with additional trips after meals and after waking from sleep. Waterproof mattress covers or washable absorbent pads should be placed under bedding and checked at each scheduled check-in. Senior cats that are incontinent or have litter box access difficulties should have a low-sided box placed directly in or immediately adjacent to their resting area. Document all accidents — frequency and character (urine only, stool, or both) — so the owner can follow up with their veterinarian if needed.

## Heat and Cold Sensitivity in Senior Pets
Senior pets have reduced thermoregulatory capacity, making them more vulnerable to both overheating and hypothermia. In warm weather, senior pets should be exercised only during the cooler parts of the day (before 9 AM and after 5 PM) and should always have access to cool fresh water and shade. Watch for early signs of heat stress: excessive panting, drooling, unsteady gait, or glazed eyes — cool the pet with damp towels and seek veterinary advice if symptoms do not resolve within minutes. In cold weather or air-conditioned facilities, provide an extra blanket and consider a pet-safe sweater for thin-coated or low-body-fat senior dogs. Never use heating pads on high settings for senior pets as reduced skin sensitivity increases the risk of burns.

## End-of-Stay Health Summary for Owners
Before an owner collects their senior pet, prepare a brief written summary covering the pet's appetite, water intake, mobility, bathroom habits, sleep quality, and any behavioral or physical changes observed during the stay. Highlight any incidents — missed meals, accidents, signs of pain, or unusual behavior — even if they appeared to resolve, so the owner can monitor for recurrence at home. If medication was administered, confirm the exact doses given and the times, and flag any doses that were difficult to give. A written summary is more reliable than a verbal handover, particularly when multi-day stays involve several staff members. Encourage owners to share the summary with their veterinarian at their next routine visit, as hotel observations can provide useful longitudinal health data.
