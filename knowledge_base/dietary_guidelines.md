# Dietary Guidelines and Restriction Management for Pet Hotels

## Honoring Owner Dietary Instructions
Always follow the owner's feeding instructions exactly. Do not substitute, augment, or modify a pet's diet without written authorization from the owner. Bring the pet's own food whenever possible — dietary changes, even brief ones, can cause digestive upset, especially in dogs and cats. If the owner did not bring enough food, contact them before substituting with a hotel supply. When a hotel-supplied food must be used, transition gradually by mixing 25% new food with 75% original food on the first day.

## Common Food Allergies and Intolerances in Dogs
The most common food allergens for dogs are: beef (most common), dairy, wheat/gluten, egg, chicken, lamb, and soy. Dogs with food allergies typically show skin itching, recurring ear infections, and gastrointestinal upset (vomiting, diarrhea). If the owner has provided an allergen-free or limited-ingredient diet, strictly prevent cross-contamination — clean food bowls thoroughly before use, do not allow the dog to eat from other pets' bowls, and avoid giving treats that are not approved by the owner.

## Common Food Allergies and Intolerances in Cats
Common feline food allergens include: beef, fish, dairy, and chicken. Food allergies in cats most commonly manifest as skin issues (miliary dermatitis, over-grooming) or chronic vomiting. Some cats are lactose intolerant — never offer cow's milk. Tuna and fish-based foods, while often preferred by cats, should not be fed daily as excessive fish intake can cause neurological issues (thiamine deficiency) and has high mercury content. If a cat is on a hydrolyzed protein or novel protein diet prescribed by a veterinarian, no other protein source may be offered.

## Prescription and Medical Diets
Prescription diets include: kidney support diets (low phosphorus, restricted protein — for cats and dogs with chronic kidney disease), urinary diets (for cats prone to bladder stones — often wet food only), hypoallergenic/hydrolyzed protein diets, weight management diets, GI support diets (highly digestible, low fat), and cardiac diets. These diets are prescribed by veterinarians and must be followed strictly. Feeding a non-prescribed food to a pet on a medical diet can undermine treatment. Never feed table scraps or unapproved treats to a pet on a prescription diet.

## Caloric Guidelines and Weight Management
Caloric needs vary significantly by species, breed, age, and activity level. As a general guide, a 10-lb dog needs approximately 200–275 calories per day; a 50-lb dog needs approximately 700–900 calories. A 10-lb cat needs approximately 200–250 calories per day. Obese pets should be fed the prescribed reduced amount — do not offer extra food or treats out of sympathy. Underweight pets may require more frequent feeding; confirm amounts and frequency with the owner. Do not compare one pet's portion size to another's — individual needs differ significantly.

## Foods Toxic to Dogs
Never feed dogs: chocolate (theobromine toxicity — dark chocolate and baking chocolate are most dangerous), xylitol (artificial sweetener found in sugar-free gum, peanut butter, and some baked goods — causes sudden hypoglycemia and liver failure), grapes and raisins (kidney failure — mechanism unknown, no safe dose), onions, garlic, leeks, and chives (damage red blood cells), macadamia nuts (neurological symptoms), avocado (vomiting and diarrhea), raw yeast dough (expands in stomach, releases alcohol), and alcohol in any form. If a dog ingests any toxic substance, contact the ASPCA Poison Control (888-426-4435) and the owner immediately.

## Foods Toxic to Cats
Never feed cats: onions, garlic, and chives (destroy red blood cells), chocolate, caffeine, alcohol, grapes and raisins, xylitol, raw fish in large amounts (thiamine deficiency), raw eggs (salmonella risk and biotin deficiency from avidin in whites), and dog food as a primary diet (lacks taurine essential for heart and eye health). Lilies (Lilium and Hemerocallis species) are extremely toxic to cats even in small amounts and cause acute kidney failure — ensure no cut flowers in cat housing areas. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is deadly to cats and must never be used.

## Water Requirements and Hydration
Dogs should drink approximately 1 ounce of water per pound of body weight per day (a 30-lb dog needs ~30 oz / ~4 cups). Cats on dry food need more water supplementation — offer wet food or a pet water fountain. Check and refill water bowls at least twice daily; once with morning feeding, once with evening feeding, and ad-lib access throughout the day. Increased water consumption in a senior pet can indicate diabetes, kidney disease, or Cushing's disease — note and report to owner. Decreased water consumption (especially in cats) combined with reduced food intake warrants same-day owner notification.

## Raw and Fresh Food Handling
Some owners feed raw (BARF — biologically appropriate raw food) diets. If handling raw food: wear gloves, use dedicated food preparation tools, and sanitize surfaces immediately after preparation. Do not allow raw-fed pets to lick other pets that may be immunocompromised. Store raw food in a separate sealed container in the refrigerator and discard uneaten portions within 30 minutes. Never refreeze thawed raw food. Staff with any open cuts or compromised immune systems should not handle raw food. Document raw feeding in the care log.

## Portion Timing for Multiple-Meal Schedules
Pets on two-meal-per-day schedules should be fed at consistent 10–12 hour intervals — a standard split is 7:00 AM and 6:00 PM. Pets on three-meal schedules should be fed at evenly spaced intervals, such as 7:00 AM, 1:00 PM, and 7:00 PM, to maintain stable blood glucose and prevent hunger-driven anxiety between meals. Puppies under 6 months typically require three to four meals per day and must not go more than 6 hours without food during waking hours. When transitioning a pet onto a new meal frequency for the hotel stay, divide the total daily caloric allowance equally across the new number of meals — do not increase total daily intake. Log the exact time each meal is served and note how much of the portion was consumed at each sitting.

## Treat Policies and Caloric Accounting
Treats must never be given to pets with food allergies, prescription diets, or weight management plans unless the owner has explicitly listed approved treats in writing. For pets without dietary restrictions, treats should not exceed 10% of the pet's total daily caloric intake; for a 30-lb dog on a 600-calorie daily budget, that is a maximum of 60 treat calories per day. All treats given must be logged in the daily care record, including the type of treat and estimated quantity. Commercial treats should have caloric information on the label; if the label is unavailable, estimate 5 calories per small treat and 15–25 calories per medium treat as a conservative default. Staff should not give treats as a comfort response to every whimper or begging behavior, as this can inadvertently reinforce anxiety and rapidly exceed daily caloric limits.

## Hydration Strategies for Dry-Food-Only Pets
Pets fed exclusively dry kibble consume very little moisture through food and must compensate through drinking — monitor and encourage water intake actively throughout the day. A useful strategy is to add 1–2 tablespoons of warm water to the dry kibble at each meal to increase moisture intake without changing the diet; confirm with the owner before doing this. Place at least two water bowls in different locations within or near the pet's housing area, as some animals drink more readily when water is not adjacent to the food bowl. For cats on dry food, a pet water fountain is strongly preferred over a static bowl — cats are drawn to moving water, and many cats on dry food are chronically mildly dehydrated. Refill and rinse water bowls at every meal and additionally check water levels at midday; do not simply top off a dirty bowl — empty, rinse, and refill completely.

## Managing Pets That Eat Too Fast
Fast eating (also called "bolting" food) increases the risk of vomiting immediately post-meal, and in large and deep-chested breeds (Great Danes, Standard Poodles, Weimaraners, Dobermans), contributes to bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), which is a life-threatening emergency. Use a slow-feeder bowl or scatter-feed kibble on a clean litter mat to force the pet to work for each bite and extend meal duration from under one minute to 5–10 minutes. Divide the total meal portion into two or three smaller servings given 5–10 minutes apart if no slow-feeder is available. Never allow a fast-eating pet to exercise, run, or engage in play for at least 45–60 minutes after a full meal. Document fast-eating behavior in the pet's care notes and notify the owner at checkout so they can consider a permanent slow-feeder solution at home.

## Food Rotation and Variety Within Dietary Restrictions
Pets with established dietary restrictions or prescription diets must not receive any foods outside those restrictions even in the name of variety — the approved diet is non-negotiable. For pets without restrictions, limited approved variety can reduce finickiness and provides mental enrichment; for example, rotating between two protein sources within the same brand on alternating days is acceptable if the owner has approved it. Never introduce a brand-new protein source (one the pet has never eaten before) during a hotel stay, as novel proteins can trigger delayed allergic responses or GI upset that are difficult to attribute correctly. If the owner provides multiple approved protein varieties to rotate, follow the rotation schedule they specify and note which protein was fed each day in the care log. Any food rotation must be documented so the owner has a complete record of what was fed during the stay.

## Post-Meal Rest Requirements
All pets should have a minimum 30-minute rest period after each meal before any high-intensity physical activity, including fetch, group play, or vigorous leash walking. For large and giant breeds, this rest window should be extended to 60 minutes minimum given the elevated risk of bloat associated with exercise immediately post-feeding. Post-meal rest should be scheduled as a structured quiet period — return the pet to their housing area with a safe enrichment item such as a stuffed Kong or chew, rather than placing them back in group play. During post-meal rest, monitor for signs of gastric discomfort: unproductive retching, a visibly distended abdomen, excessive drooling, and restlessness are all emergency indicators in large dogs. Cats should also rest after meals; a cat that vomits immediately or shortly after eating may be eating too fast, too much, or from an elevated bowl — adjust feeding conditions before the next meal.
