# Medication Administration Guidelines for Pet Hotels

## Intake Medication Review
At check-in, obtain a complete and signed medication list from the owner that includes: medication name, dose amount, route of administration (oral, topical, injectable, eye/ear drop), frequency, timing (with food or without, specific times), storage requirements, and the condition being treated. Never administer a medication not on the signed intake form. If any instructions are unclear, call the owner before administering. Photograph the medication label and intake form and file with the pet's record.

## Oral Medication Administration
Most oral medications can be given with a small amount of food (a pill pocket, soft treat, or piece of wet food) to increase compliance. Some medications must be given on an empty stomach — confirm with owner or medication label. Never crush extended-release tablets unless specifically instructed by a veterinarian. Liquid medications should be drawn up in a syringe and administered slowly into the side of the mouth (between the cheek and teeth) to prevent choking. Always offer water after administering oral medication. Document the time administered immediately after giving the dose.

## Timing Consistency for Medications
Timing consistency is critical for medications managing chronic conditions. Anti-seizure medications must be given within 1 hour of the scheduled time. Insulin for diabetic pets must be given within 30 minutes of the scheduled feeding time and never administered if the pet has not eaten. Thyroid medications for cats (methimazole) should be given at consistent 12-hour intervals. Antibiotics are most effective when given at evenly spaced intervals. Set a phone reminder or schedule-based alert for every pet on medication to ensure no doses are missed.

## Eye and Ear Drop Administration
For eye drops: gently hold the eye open, tilt the pet's head back slightly, and apply drops to the inner corner of the eye without touching the dropper to the eye surface. Gently hold the eye closed for 30 seconds. For ear drops: gently fold back the ear flap, apply the prescribed number of drops into the ear canal, then massage the base of the ear for 30 seconds to distribute the medication. Do not insert applicator tips deeply into the ear canal. Always use separate applicators for each ear if treating both to prevent cross-contamination.

## Topical Medication Administration
Apply topical medications (flea treatments, anti-fungal creams, wound sprays) exactly as directed on the intake form. For skin conditions, part the fur to ensure the medication reaches the skin. Prevent the animal from licking topical medications for at least 10–15 minutes after application; use an e-collar (cone) if the pet persistently licks. Wear gloves when applying any topical medication as many can be absorbed through human skin. Never apply a dog flea/tick product to a cat — pyrethrins and permethrins found in dog-specific products are toxic to cats.

## Injection Administration (Staff with Proper Training Only)
Insulin injections for diabetic pets should only be administered by trained staff. Gather supplies: correct insulin vial (never shake insulin — roll gently), correct syringe size, alcohol wipe. Draw up the exact prescribed dose. Inject subcutaneously (under the skin, not into muscle) typically at the scruff of the neck or behind the shoulder blade — rotate injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy. Document dose administered, injection site, and time. If the pet will not eat, do not administer insulin — contact owner immediately.

## Medication Storage Requirements
Store all medications in a locked, labeled cabinet. Insulin must be refrigerated (36–46°F) and protected from light and freezing. Some liquid antibiotics require refrigeration after mixing. Check expiration dates at intake and flag any expired medications to the owner before starting administration. Do not store multiple pets' medications together without clear individual labeling. Controlled substances (phenobarbital, tramadol, gabapentin) require secure locked storage separate from other medications.

## Missed Doses and Emergencies
If a dose is missed by less than 2 hours, administer it as soon as remembered unless it is almost time for the next dose — never double-dose. For medications where timing is critical (insulin, anti-seizure drugs), always contact the owner when a dose is delayed or missed. If a pet vomits within 20 minutes of receiving oral medication, contact the owner about whether to re-administer. In a medication emergency (overdose, wrong medication given, severe adverse reaction), contact a veterinarian or animal poison control immediately and document everything.

## Documentation Requirements
Maintain a medication log for every pet with: date, time, medication name, dose given, route, who administered it, and any observations (pet refused, vomited, showed reaction). This log must be reviewed by staff at every shift change. Provide the completed medication log to the owner at checkout. Document any refusal to take medication, side effects observed, or concerns immediately and notify the owner within the same business day.
